Research

Gene prediction is the process of identifying genes - regions of DNA sequence in a genome which encode cellular products. Although most biologists are inclined to assume that gene prediction is a solved problem, this assumption is indeed not grounded in reality, save for a handful of model organisms. While prokaryotic gene prediction is also flawed, its extent far pales in comparison to that of eukaryotes. This is mainly due to: (i) eukaryotic genomes are far more complicated, and (ii) some eukaryotic lineages are too divergent for homology to be meaningful. To make matters worse, the machine learning models integral to gene prediction has remained largely stagnant for the past 25 years. Such stagnation may be indicative of a potential theoretical limit to their modelling power, regardless of sample size.

To put simply, this false assumption of gene prediction has been perpetuating a vicious cycle, where models trained on poor predictions cause more poor predictions, which are then put into databases for training new models. As you can imagine, this propagation of error has far-reaching consequences for downstream research, especially those that concern human health.

My research aims to solve gene prediction to a satisfactory level, with a focus on eukaryotic protein-coding genes (baby steps!).
